Product Essentials is an upskilling experience — not a rerun of new-hire Day 1, and not a feature tour. It's designed to strengthen how learners use Thumbtack product knowledge in real conversations. This version is being piloted with Sales first.

TL;DR for Delivery: success here depends on a shift from product knowledge to product use. If the live gets delivered as a recap, learners may leave feeling like they "attended training" but not noticeably more capable.

What it's for

Help learners connect product pieces, explain them clearly, and use product understanding to guide better judgment in real conversations.

For Sales: ROI conversations, weekly budget framing, visibility vs. being chosen, stronger discovery, sharper coaching on what affects pro success.

What it is not

  • A repeat of Day 1 product training
  • A lecture-heavy product overview
  • A feature memorization exercise
  • A standalone Sales methodology course
  • A replacement for live coaching, QA, or manager reinforcement

How it's built

Most of the async courses come from Product Day 1. Several pieces that were previously live in Day 1 have been converted to async courses here, since these learners are already on phones and can't be pulled away as often as new hires.

That leaves one live session in Product Essentials: Product in Practice. Calibrate it for application, not first exposure — learners bring product foundation from the async.

The bigger picture (so Delivery can answer questions)

The live session is one piece of a connected program. Delivery isn't running these other pieces, but should know they exist — they shape what learners bring into the room and what managers reinforce afterward.

  • Two new Sales-specific courses — Beyond the Checklist: Diagnostic Discovery and From Hesitation to Commitment. Built specifically for tenured AEs.
  • Four-persona AI simulation arc inside Beyond the Checklist — Maria, Devon, Linda (practice), then Marcus (scored capstone). Each produces a manager-shareable scorecard.
  • Manager-first sequencing — managers go through Product Essentials before their teams, so they can coach the content. This is why the Slack channel adds managers Monday before learners.
  • Sales Manager Discussion Guide (Coda) — coaching companion that turns simulation scorecards and live-session attendance into structured 1:1 conversations. (See the Manager Guide tab for the link.)
  • Async + Slack + live as a connected loop — not three separate experiences. The What We Saw This Week section in the live deck pulls directly from Slack responses (more on the Slack Layer tab).

Product in Practice is the live application session inside Product Essentials. It's built around coaching and analysis — not lecture. The session helps learners use product knowledge in higher-value conversations, with a Product SME on hand for the harder edge-case questions.

The session feels like: "Let's slow down this moment and look at what a stronger move would be." Not: "Let me reteach all the content you should have already learned."

What the session is

For Sales, focuses on how product understanding shows up in:

  • Weekly budget hesitation
  • ROI pushback
  • Preventable late-stage objections
  • Stronger consultative reasoning
  • Earlier surfacing of what's shaping the pro's concern

What it is not

  • A basic product recap
  • A read-through of course content
  • A slide lecture
  • A generic discussion of "best practices"
  • A session where the trainer gives the answer too early
SME

Product SME role

The SME is part of the design — not optional background support. They're there to handle the harder questions that come up once learners try to apply the content.

  • Unexpected product behavior
  • Hard-to-explain customer or pro experiences
  • Edge cases that came up on live calls

Delivery's job for the SME

  • Make sure the SME is invited and has access
  • Frame the SME segment clearly for learners
  • Keep questions specific and tied to real work
  • Route higher-complexity questions to the SME — don't guess

The Delivery Team's job is not just to "present" Product Essentials. The role is to preserve the design intent — and the live session is where most of that intent succeeds or breaks down.

In the live, do

  • Open clearly and efficiently
  • Assume product foundation is already in place
  • Use slides as prompts for thinking, not lecture
  • Coach toward better diagnosis, not just better wording
  • Stay grounded in what the pro is really reacting to
  • Grade Activity 1 in From Hesitation to Commitment using the rubric already built in Compass
  • Draft a Slack welcome message any time managers or learners are added to the channel
  • Pull in the SME for higher-complexity product questions
  • Review Slack responses before the live
  • Enter Slack feedback into What We Saw This Week
  • Add anything else they want in the Coda doc — examples, follow-up questions, reminders (speaker notes are already done)

Don't

  • Reteach the full product curriculum
  • Turn the live into a lecture
  • Rush to the "best answer" before learners think
  • Drift into unrelated objection handling or generic Sales talk
  • Flatten coaching into script rewrites only
  • Guess on edge cases when the SME should be brought in
  • Treat the Slack layer as separate from the live
  • Treat the Coda doc as a required deliverable — it's a working tool, not a checkbox
Graded assignment — Trainers own the grading. In the From Hesitation to Commitment course, Activity 1 is a graded assignment. For now, Trainers grade it directly in Compass — not managers.
Trainer-led

Why Trainers, not managers (for now)

Eventually we may want managers grading their own people, but that would require training every manager on how to grade within Compass — which isn't a lift we're taking on right now.

Keeping grading with Trainers means we get consistent calibration, faster turnaround, and a cleaner feedback loop while we're still in pilot. The manager option stays open for future versions.

Already built

The rubric is already in place

Activity 1 has custom grade criteria already built into the Compass exercise settings — Trainers will be able to pass or fail learners against the existing rubric without needing to invent one.

  • Open the activity in Compass and review the rubric in advance
  • Use it as-is — it's been written for this exact assignment
  • Add any supplementary materials Delivery wants alongside it
  • Test it during the manager dry-run week to get comfortable with how the criteria apply

What learners are doing in Activity 1

Learners are shown three soft asks overheard in real calls (each with context on what the pro said earlier). They identify what makes each one soft, then pick one to rewrite as a stronger ask anchored to the pro's stated outcome — and submit the rewrite for Trainer review.

The three custom grade criteria (built into Compass)

Criterion 01 · Outcome anchoring
The rewrite ties the ask to the specific outcome the pro stated in the context.
Did the learner connect the budget ask to what the pro actually said they wanted? (e.g., fully booked through fall, 3 more bookings/month, hiring a second employee within 6 months.)
Criterion 02 · Specific number + rationale
The rewrite includes a specific number with a rationale, no hedging language.
No "maybe," "could try," "around," or "what do you think?" — a concrete number, with reasoning that ties to the outcome.
Criterion 03 · AE owns the recommendation
The rewrite makes a recommendation — the AE owns the number, the AE doesn't ask the pro to set it.
Catches the move from "what do you think?" framing to a consultative recommendation the AE stands behind.

Pass / fail is straightforward: a strong rewrite hits all three. A weak one hedges the number, ignores the stated outcome, or punts the decision back to the pro.

The Sales Manager Discussion Guide is the connective tissue between Product Essentials and what happens in 1:1 coaching. Delivery doesn't run it — but Delivery should know what's in it, when to point managers to it, and how it fits the broader program design.

Why this matters for Delivery: past programs have asked managers to "reinforce content" without giving them tools to do it. The Manager Guide makes that job concrete. When questions come up in the live ("how should my manager follow up on this?"), Delivery can point straight to the relevant section.
What's in it

Course-by-course coaching prompts

For each Product Essentials course, the guide includes 1:1 questions managers can ask to check application, not just completion. Instead of "Did you finish the course?", managers ask "Walk me through how you'd explain weekly budget to a pro who's hesitating."

Pass / fail conversation starters

Different rubric outcomes call for different coaching moves. The guide has prompts for 3/3, 2/3, 1/3, and Did Not Complete results — so managers know how to celebrate strong attempts and how to coach struggle without it feeling punitive.

Live session debrief prompts

Follow-up questions managers can use after their team attends Product in Practice — turning the live into individual coaching, not just attendance.

The big one

Simulation-specific coaching paths

Beyond the Checklist includes a four-persona AI simulation arc — Maria, Devon, Linda, and the scored Marcus capstone. Each one produces a manager-shareable scorecard with the AE's typed responses.

The Manager Guide gives managers a structured 1:1 path tied to each simulation's rubric. When an AE sends their scorecard via Slack, the manager already has the coaching framework — they don't need to invent it.

Why Coda, not a PDF

It's a living document — searchable, linkable, referenceable in real time during 1:1s. As scorecards come in via Slack, managers can pull up the relevant section in seconds.

Post-pilot

Cadence & cohort size

The plan is to run Product in Practice on a biweekly cadence with the Sales team, originally.

Cohort size is still being calibrated. The working goal is a minimum of 10 learners per live session — enough to get genuine discussion and pattern-spotting in the room without overwhelming the coaching dynamic.

Slack automation (in test)

I'm testing an automation for the weekly Slack prompts so they don't depend on a human remembering to send them. Two paths under consideration:

  • Claude-delivered — generates and posts the prompt on cadence
  • Native Slack reminders — simpler, scheduled, no model in the loop

Either way, the goal is consistency: the social-learning layer only works if the prompts actually show up week after week.

Continuity

One persistent Slack channel

After the pilot, we should keep using the same Slack channel and just keep adding new cohorts to it — not spin up a fresh channel each time.

Why this matters from an ID lens:

  • Just-in-time learning — if a learner has a product question after training ends, they have a place to ask it
  • Continuous reinforcement — product fluency builds over months, not in one session
  • Community of practice — earlier cohorts model the kind of reasoning newer cohorts are working toward
  • Searchable history — past prompts, answers, and edge cases become a living reference
  • Lower drop-off — keeps the learning surface alive instead of ending it at the live session
